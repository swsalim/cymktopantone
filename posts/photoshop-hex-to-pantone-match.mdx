You may already know how to move **CMYK → Pantone** in Photoshop from our <Link href="/blog/cmyk-pantone-photoshop">CMYK to Pantone in Photoshop</Link> walkthrough. A different—and very common—search is:

**“I have a HEX from the web or a style guide. How do I find the closest Pantone in Photoshop?”**

Photoshop can get you **into Pantone libraries** from a picked color, but the **truth test** for brand work is still **HEX → matcher → swatch book → proof**. Below is the full loop.

## When HEX is the source of truth

Web and product teams often ship **#RRGGBB** values. Print vendors still speak **PMS + coated/uncoated**. Your job in Photoshop is to:

1. **Enter or sample** the HEX accurately in the document’s working color space.
2. **Find the closest library color** (when Adobe’s Pantone data is available).
3. **Log both HEX and PMS** so downstream files do not fork into mystery reds.

## Step 1 — Put the HEX into the Color Picker

### Type it directly

1. Double-click the **foreground color swatch** in the toolbar.
2. In the **Color Picker**, choose the **RGB** or **HEX** entry mode (depends on version; HEX is often visible as **`#`** field).
3. Paste **#** plus six digits. Photoshop updates the **RGB** sliders to match.

### Sample from a pixel layer

1. Select the **Eyedropper Tool**.
2. Set **Sample Size** to **Point Sample** when you need exact single-pixel values, or **3×3 Average** on noisy gradients.
3. Click the pixel, then reopen the **Color Picker** to read **HEX**.

**Caution:** **sRGB** HEX in a document that uses a different **RGB working space** will **not** magically become “more accurate Pantone.” For packaging handoffs, align **Edit → Color Settings** with what your studio agrees on, and **communicate the profile** on the ticket.

## Step 2 — Jump to Pantone Color Libraries

With the Color Picker open on your color:

1. Click **Color Libraries…** (sometimes labeled similarly in older builds).
2. Choose the **Pantone book** your printer named—usually **PANTONE + Solid Coated** for **C** specs.
3. Read the **highlighted swatch** as Photoshop’s **closest book neighbor** to the RGB/HEX you entered.

**If libraries are missing:** Current Adobe versions may require **Pantone Connect**. While access is sorted out, use <Link href="/convert-hex-to-pantone-pms">HEX to Pantone</Link> on CMYK Pantone—it stays available in the browser and uses the same family of **perceptual matching** as the rest of the site.

## Step 3 — Save the swatch for reuse

1. With the Pantone color active, open **Window → Swatches**.
2. Click **New Swatch** and name it with the **PMS code** (example: `PANTONE 185 C`).

That gives art directors a **one-click** swatch for touch-ups without re-typing HEX every time.

## Step 4 — Verify with an online matcher (recommended)

Photoshop’s library pick and an online matcher **usually agree**, but not always—different **rounding**, **library versions**, and **in-gamut mapping** cause small disagreements.

**Workflow:**

1. Copy **HEX without #** (for example `DA291C`).
2. Open <Link href="/convert-hex-to-pantone-pms">HEX to Pantone</Link> and scan the **top matches** and **percentages**.
3. If Photoshop and the site diverge, **do not guess**—hold a **physical swatch** next to a **calibrated proof** and let the printer advise.

## Step 5 — Reverse check when Pantone is already specified

If the brand bible says **PANTONE 293 C** but your **web HEX** feels off:

1. Use <Link href="/pantone">Pantone color lookup</Link> for **HEX/RGB** approximations of **293 C**.
2. Type those into Photoshop to see **on-screen alignment** with marketing’s CSS.

Again: **screen ≠ ink**, but this stops teams from using **three different blues** that all claim to be “brand.”

## HEX-only vs CMYK-first Photoshop workflows

- **UI / web comps:** HEX and **sRGB** lead. Pantone is **documentation** for print partners.
- **Print retouching:** **CMYK** or **spot channels** may lead. Use <Link href="/convert-cmyk-to-pantone-pms">CMYK to Pantone</Link> when the brief gives **ink percentages** instead of HEX.
