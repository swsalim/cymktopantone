Canva is built for **speed**: social posts, decks, one-off flyers. It is **not** a replacement for **Adobe + Pantone Connect + a swatch book** when a packaging buyer asks, **“What Pantone is this orange?”**

Still, thousands of teams **start** in Canva. The realistic workflow is:

1. **Extract a reliable HEX** (or document the brand kit values).
2. **Match HEX → closest Pantone** with a dedicated tool.
3. **Confirm on press** with your printer—not with Canva’s preview alone.

That is what this guide covers.

## Where Canva shows color values

### Default color picker (single design)

1. Click the element whose color you need (shape, text, background).
2. Open the **color tile** in the top or side toolbar.
3. Switch to the **HEX** field if it is not already visible (UI varies slightly between **Canva Web** and **mobile**).

Copy the **six-digit HEX** (with or without `#`). If you only see a **hue slider** and **palette chips**, use **Document colors** or the **+** under **Brand kit** (paid plans) to pin exact values.

### Brand Kit (Teams / Pro workflows)

If your organization uses **Brand Kit**:

1. Open **Brand Hub** (wording may vary) and locate **Brand colors**.
2. Copy each **HEX** as defined by the brand owner—**do not** re-sample from compressed JPEG exports; JPEG artifacts will lie to you.

**Why HEX first?** Canva’s interface centers on **screen sRGB**. Pantone is an **ink system**. The honest bridge is: **sRGB HEX → closest PMS approximation → physical proof**.

## Match HEX to Pantone with a converter

Paste your HEX into <Link href="/convert-hex-to-pantone-pms">HEX to Pantone</Link>. You will get **ordered Pantone suggestions** with **match percentages**.

**How to use the results in an email to a printer:**

> “Our Canva brand orange is **#FF6600** on screen. Closest coated PMS candidates are **[codes from tool]**. Please confirm against **Pantone Solid Coated** before plating.”

That sentence saves a round trip of guesswork.

## If you have several Canva brand colors

Do not repeat the workflow six times in a row unless you enjoy suffering.

1. List all HEX values in a note (one per line).
2. Open <Link href="/brand-palette-to-pantone">Brand palette to Pantone</Link>.
3. Paste, click **Build table**, then **Copy CSV** or **Markdown** for your **style guide** or **vendor packet**.

Share the **same export** with marketing so **social templates** and **print** stop inventing different oranges.

## Export pitfalls that break color consistency

- **PNG/JPEG downloads** recompress colors. Do not **eyedropper** the export to “recover” brand HEX unless you have no other choice.
- **PDF from Canva** is handy for **soft proofing layout**, not for **final separations** on every press. Complex spot workflows still land in **InDesign / Illustrator** with explicit **spot swatches**—see <Link href="/blog/indesign-pantone-spot-color-pdf">Pantone spot colors in InDesign</Link>.
- **Different monitors** change how you *feel* about a match. Numbers travel better than memory.

## When the client says “use Pantone 185 C” but Canva looks wrong

Do not fight the slider. **Lock to the spec:**

1. Look up **185 C** in <Link href="/pantone">Pantone color lookup</Link> for **HEX/RGB** approximations you can type into Canva for **closer screen alignment**.
2. Remember: **on-screen** is still **sRGB**, not ink. The **printer’s proof** is the referee.

## Short checklist

- HEX copied from **Brand Kit** or the **color panel**, not from a **compressed export**.
- <Link href="/convert-hex-to-pantone-pms">HEX to Pantone</Link> run for **each new color** without an existing PMS spec.
- **Palette table** exported when there are **three or more** primaries.
- **Printer sign-off** for anything that ships to **shelves, apparel, or signage**.
